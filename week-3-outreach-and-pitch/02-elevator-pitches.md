# Module 3.2 — Elevator Pitches

**Reading Time:** 18 minutes
**Objective:** Develop multiple pitch formats so you can describe the AI OS Partner Program clearly and compellingly in any situation — from a 30-second elevator ride to a 60-second networking conversation.

---

## Why Pitches Matter More Than You Think

You will be asked "What do you do?" hundreds of times. At networking events. At your kid's soccer game. At a bar. In a DM. On a Zoom call. In a cold email.

Every single one of those is a chance to plant a seed.

The problem is, most people answer with something like: "We do AI consulting for small businesses." That's accurate. It's also forgettable. It sounds like 10,000 other companies. It creates zero curiosity.

A great pitch does three things:
1. **Creates a mental image** — they can see what you do
2. **Triggers a question** — they want to know more
3. **Is easy to repeat** — they can tell someone else about you

If your pitch doesn't do all three, it's not a pitch. It's a label.

---

## The 30-Second Pitch (Networking / Elevator)

This is your bread and butter. Use it at events, casual conversations, and anytime someone asks what you do and you have less than a minute.

> "We build AI operating systems for businesses doing $1 to $10 million. Think of it as a digital chief of staff — it knows your numbers, handles your follow-ups, creates your content, and gives you a briefing every morning. Our clients typically get 20+ hours a week back within 90 days."

### Why This Works

- **"AI operating systems"** — not "AI consulting" or "automation." An operating system sounds essential, foundational, permanent. It's something you need, not something you might want.
- **"$1 to $10 million"** — specificity. It signals you have a niche, you know your market, and you're not just working with anyone who breathes.
- **"Digital chief of staff"** — analogy. Everyone understands what a chief of staff does. Now they can picture what the AI OS does without needing a technical explanation.
- **"Knows your numbers, handles your follow-ups, creates your content, gives you a briefing"** — four concrete things. Not vague "streamlines operations." Specific, tangible outcomes.
- **"20+ hours a week back within 90 days"** — measurable. Believable. Desirable.

### The Follow-Up Question Test

After delivering this pitch, 9 out of 10 people will ask some version of:
- "How does that work?"
- "What kind of businesses?"
- "How much does that cost?"

That's the signal it worked. You created curiosity. Now you transition to the 60-second version.

## The 60-Second Pitch (When They're Interested)

Use this when someone leans in after the 30-second version, or when you have a full minute in a more structured setting (BNI, pitch competitions, intro calls).

> "We build AI operating systems for businesses doing $1 to $10 million — typically service businesses, contractors, agencies, and nonprofits.
>
> Most owners we talk to are spending 20, 30, sometimes 40 hours a week on stuff that doesn't directly grow the business — follow-ups, scheduling, content creation, reporting, chasing down information. They know AI could help but they don't know where to start, and ChatGPT alone doesn't cut it.
>
> What we do is build a system that handles all of that automatically. It knows your team, your clients, your metrics, your strategy. It creates content in your voice, sends follow-ups on time, and gives you a morning briefing so you know exactly where everything stands.
>
> We just did this for a lightning protection company in Florida — three locations, 22 target keywords. Within 90 days, 17 of those keywords hit page one, and their owner said it was like hiring three people without the payroll.
>
> The entry point is a $15,000 Architecture phase where we design the entire system. If you know any business owners drowning in busywork, I'd love an introduction."

### Breakdown

| Element | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| Market definition | Establishes who this is for |
| Pain identification | Gets them nodding — "that's me" or "I know someone like that" |
| Solution description | Concrete outcomes, not features |
| Social proof | Real client, real result, real specificity |
| CTA with referral hook | Doesn't ask them to buy — asks them to connect you |

### The Referral Hook

Notice the close: **"If you know any business owners drowning in busywork, I'd love an introduction."**

This is deliberate. At networking events, you're rarely talking to your ideal client. But you might be talking to someone who knows them. By ending with a referral ask instead of a sales ask, you:
- Remove pressure from the person you're talking to
- Turn every conversation into a potential pipeline source
- Sound like a professional, not a desperate salesperson

## The "Party Pitch" (Casual Social Settings)

When someone at a barbecue, wedding, or kid's birthday party asks "What do you do?" — the last thing you want is to sound like you're selling.

This version is relaxed, conversational, and curiosity-driven:

> "I help business owners get their time back using AI. You know how most owners spend half their week on stuff that doesn't make them money — follow-ups, scheduling, posting on social media, pulling reports? We automate all of that so they can actually focus on growing the business."

**If they ask more:** "We basically build an AI operating system that runs the day-to-day. One of our clients said it's like having a chief of staff that never sleeps and never calls in sick."

**If they don't seem interested:** Stop. Smile. Change the subject. Never be the person who pitches at parties when nobody asked for details.

### Reading the Room

The party pitch has a built-in escape hatch. If their eyes glaze over after the first sentence, you pivot. "Anyway — how do you know [host name]?" No damage done.

If they lean in and start asking questions, you have permission to share more. But even then, keep it to 2-3 exchanges. Then: "I'd love to tell you more — shoot me a text and we can grab coffee." Take it offline. Parties are for planting seeds, not closing deals.

## The Comparison Pitch (When They Say "I Already Use ChatGPT")

You will hear this constantly. Every business owner has tried ChatGPT at least once. Most of them think that means they "have AI." This pitch reframes the conversation:

> "ChatGPT is a tool. The AI OS is a system. Here's the difference: every time you open ChatGPT, you start from scratch. You have to explain your business, your context, your goals — every single time. It doesn't know your team, your metrics, your schedule, or your strategy.
>
> The AI OS already knows all of that. It runs in the background. It sends follow-ups without you asking. It creates content in your voice. It pulls your data together and tells you what to pay attention to. It's the difference between having a calculator and having an accountant."

### The Calculator vs. Accountant Analogy

This is one of the most effective reframes in your toolkit. Everyone understands the difference between a calculator and an accountant:

- A **calculator** requires you to know what to calculate. You do the work. It's a tool.
- An **accountant** knows your situation, proactively manages your finances, and tells you things you didn't know to ask. It's a system.

ChatGPT = calculator. AI OS = accountant. Simple. Memorable. Shareable.

### Handling the "But AI is Free" Objection

> "You're right — the tools are accessible. But that's like saying lumber is available so anyone can build a house. The value isn't in the tools. It's in the architecture — knowing how to connect everything, making it work together, and building it specifically for your business. That's what the $15K Architecture phase is."

Sometimes you'll get asked directly how you're different from other agencies or consultants. Here's the response:

> "Three things. First, we don't sell monthly retainers as the entry point. Second, everything we build is custom to your business — not a template, not a one-size-fits-all playbook. Third, our CEO Corey Reeder works directly with every partner. You're not getting handed off to a junior account manager."

### The Differentiators, Ranked

| Differentiator | Why It Matters |
|---------------|---------------|
| Architecture-first model | Low risk — they see the plan before committing to build |
| Custom to their business | Not a template — the system knows THEIR operations |
| CEO involvement | Personal touch at a time when most agencies outsource everything |
| Real client results | Fox Valley, All South — live dashboards, real metrics |
| AI OS vs. one-off projects | Ongoing system, not a campaign that ends in 90 days |

## Pitch Practice Framework

Knowing the pitches is step one. Delivering them naturally is the real skill.

### Step 1: Record Yourself

Use your phone. Deliver the 30-second pitch. Play it back. Ask yourself:
- Did I sound like I was reading, or like I was talking to a friend?
- Did I rush? (Most people go too fast when nervous)
- Did my voice go up at the end of sentences? (Fix that)
- Did I pause after key points? (Let them land)

### Step 2: Remove All Jargon

Go through your pitch word by word. If your grandmother wouldn't understand a word, cut it or replace it.

| Jargon | Replace With |
|--------|-------------|
| "Leverage AI capabilities" | "Use AI to..." |
| "Streamline workflows" | "Cut the busywork" |
| "Omnichannel strategy" | "Show up everywhere your customers are" |
| "Digital transformation" | "Move your business into the modern age" |
| "Scalable solution" | "A system that grows with you" |
| "ROI-driven" | "You'll see the money come back" |
| "Synergy" | Never say this word |

### Step 3: Add Specificity

Generic statements are forgettable. Specific statements are believable.

| Generic | Specific |
|---------|----------|
| "We help businesses" | "We work with plumbing companies in the Midwest" |
| "Save you time" | "Get 20 hours a week back" |
| "Improve your marketing" | "Go from 4 blog posts a month to 36" |
| "Good results" | "17 out of 22 keywords on page one" |
| "Our clients love us" | "Fox Valley's owner said it's like hiring three people" |

**SALES PSYCHOLOGY:** Specificity creates believability. "20 hours per week" is 10x more convincing than "save time." Numbers, names, and outcomes stick in people's minds. Vague claims don't.

### Step 4: Practice the Transition

The pitch isn't the end of the conversation — it's the beginning. Practice what happens AFTER you deliver the pitch:

**If they ask a question:** Answer briefly, then redirect to a meeting. "Great question — that's exactly the kind of thing we cover in a discovery call. Want to grab 20 minutes this week?"

**If they nod but don't say anything:** Ask an open-ended question. "Does any of that resonate with what you're dealing with?"

**If they immediately say "how much?":** Don't quote the full pricing on the spot. "It depends on the scope, but the starting point is $15K for the Architecture phase. The best way to see if it makes sense is a quick call."

**If they say "I know someone who needs this":** "That's awesome — would you be comfortable making an intro? I'll make sure to take great care of them."

## Common Pitch Mistakes

### 1. Leading with Features

**Wrong:** "We use n8n for automation, Airtable for CRM, and Claude for AI content generation."
**Right:** "We automate your follow-ups, content, and reporting so you get 20 hours back."

Nobody cares about your tools. They care about their outcomes.

### 2. Trying to Close in the Pitch

**Wrong:** "So can I send you a proposal?"
**Right:** "Would it be worth a 20-minute call to see if this fits?"

The pitch opens the door. The discovery call closes it. Don't skip steps.

### 3. Talking Too Long

If you're past 60 seconds without them talking, you've lost them. The best pitches are short enough to create a conversation, not long enough to be a monologue.

### 4. Sounding Rehearsed

If it sounds memorized, it sounds fake. The framework should be memorized. The delivery should be natural. Practice until the words come out like you're explaining it to a friend over coffee.

### 5. Using the Same Pitch for Everyone

A plumber and a nonprofit director have completely different pain points. If you pitch both of them the same way, one of them (probably both) won't connect with it. Know your audience. Adapt.

## Key Takeaway

You don't need 15 minutes to explain what we do. You need 30 seconds. The goal of every pitch is to create enough curiosity that they want to hear more — whether that's a follow-up question, a meeting, or a referral. Specificity beats generality. Outcomes beat features. Conversations beat monologues. Master the 30-second version first. Everything else builds from there.

## What to Practice Today

1. **Deliver the 30-second pitch 10 times** — out loud, not in your head. Record the last 3 attempts and compare them to the first.
2. **Write your own industry-specific pitch** for a vertical you know well. Use the template but make it yours.
3. **Practice the "ChatGPT comparison" pitch** — this will come up in almost every conversation. You need it smooth and automatic.
4. **Find one piece of jargon** in your natural speaking style and replace it with plain language. Keep a running list.
