# Module 3.6 — Proposal and Close

**Reading Time:** 18 minutes
**Objective:** Learn how to create, deliver, and follow up on proposals that convert discovery and presentation conversations into signed Architecture phase contracts.

---

## The Proposal Is a Mirror, Not a Brochure

Most agencies send the same proposal to every prospect. They swap out the name, change the logo, and call it "customized." Prospects can tell. It feels generic because it is generic.

Your proposal should read like a conversation recap — because that's exactly what it is. Every section should reference something the prospect actually said. Every solution should connect to a pain they actually have. If someone read your proposal with no context, they should be able to describe the prospect's business and problems in detail.

That's the standard. A proposal that mirrors the prospect's reality converts. A proposal that talks about your agency doesn't.

---

## Proposal Structure

The proposal has 6 sections. Each one has a specific purpose. Don't skip any.

### Section 1: Their Situation (2-3 Paragraphs)

Start with them, not with you. Show that you listened, you understand, and you've been thinking about their business since the discovery call.

**Example:**

> **About [Business Name]**
>
> [Business Name] is a [industry] company based in [location] with [X employees / X locations]. Over the past [X years], [Owner Name] has built a strong reputation — [specific proof: reviews, BBB rating, community presence, revenue milestone].
>
> Right now, the biggest challenge isn't the work itself — it's everything around it. [Owner Name] is spending approximately [X] hours per week on administrative tasks, follow-ups, and content that don't directly drive revenue. Lead follow-up is inconsistent, with potential customers waiting [X hours/days] for a response. Content creation — social media, blog posts, email — has stalled because there's no system and no time.
>
> The result: a business that's operationally strong but digitally invisible, and an owner who's working in the business instead of on it.

**Key principles:**
- Use specific numbers from discovery (hours wasted, leads lost, revenue estimates)
- Quote their own words where possible: "As [Owner Name] put it during our call, '[exact quote].'"
- Don't exaggerate or editorialize. Be accurate. Accuracy builds trust.

### Section 2: The Opportunity (1-2 Paragraphs)

Now bridge from where they are to where they could be. This section should make them feel hopeful and motivated.

> **The Opportunity**
>
> [Business Name] is at an inflection point. The foundation is strong — the reputation, the team, the client base — but the systems haven't caught up. The good news is, nearly everything consuming [Owner Name]'s time can be automated, systematized, or handled by AI.
>
> Based on our analysis, implementing an AI Operating System for [Business Name] would recover an estimated 20+ hours per week in owner time, ensure every lead receives a response within 60 seconds, and generate consistent content — 9+ pieces per week — without any manual effort. The projected first-year impact: $[X] in recovered revenue from faster lead response, and $[X] in time value returned to the business.

### Section 3: Our Approach — The Architecture Phase

This is the meat. Explain what the Architecture phase includes, how it works, and what they'll receive.

> **Our Approach: The Architecture Phase**
>
> The AI OS Architecture phase is a comprehensive, CEO-led engagement where we design the entire AI operating system for [Business Name]. This is not a cookie-cutter playbook — it's a custom blueprint built specifically for your business, your team, your operations, and your goals.
>
> **What's Included:**
>
> **Week 1-2: Deep Discovery and System Mapping**
> - Full operational audit of [Business Name]
> - Team workflow analysis (who does what, where things break down)
> - Data source inventory (CRM, Google, social, financials)
> - Competitor and market positioning analysis
>
> **Week 3-4: AI System Design**
> - Custom workflow architecture (automations, triggers, data flows)
> - Content system design (topics, cadence, voice, channels)
> - Lead management system blueprint (capture, response, follow-up, close)
> - Client communication framework
> - Reporting dashboard design
>
> **Week 5-6: Blueprint Presentation and Handoff**
> - Complete AI OS Blueprint document
> - Visual system architecture (you can see exactly how everything connects)
> - Implementation roadmap with phase-by-phase timeline
> - 90-minute presentation walking you through every component
> - Q&A and refinement based on your feedback
>
> **What You Walk Away With:**
> - A complete, custom-designed AI operating system blueprint
> - Clear understanding of every workflow and automation
> - A decision-ready roadmap for Installation
> - Full ownership of the blueprint (it's yours regardless of next steps)

### Section 4: Investment

Present the price cleanly and confidently. No apologizing. No qualifying language like "we understand this is a significant investment." Just state it.

> **Investment**
>
> | Phase | Investment | Timeline |
> |-------|-----------|----------|
> | Architecture | $15,000 | 4-6 weeks |
> | Installation (if approved) | $25,000 - $75,000 | 6-12 weeks |
> | Management (optional) | $2,500 - $5,000/month | Ongoing |
>
> **Architecture Payment Structure:**
> - 50% upon contract signing: $7,500
> - 50% upon blueprint delivery: $7,500
>
> The Architecture phase is a standalone engagement. There is no obligation to proceed to Installation. You see the full system design before making any further commitment.

### Section 5: Timeline

Give them a week-by-week visual of what happens after they say yes.

> **Timeline**
>
> | Week | Activity |
> |------|----------|
> | Week 1 | Kickoff call (90 min) + operational audit begins |
> | Week 2 | Team interviews + workflow mapping |
> | Week 3 | AI system design begins |
> | Week 4 | Content system + lead management design |
> | Week 5 | Blueprint assembly + internal review |
> | Week 6 | Blueprint presentation + Q&A + refinement |
>
> *Timeline begins upon receipt of 50% deposit and completion of kickoff questionnaire.*

### Section 6: What's Next

Make the next step absurdly clear and simple.

> **What's Next**
>
> If you're ready to move forward, here's all you need to do:
>
> 1. Reply to this email with "Let's go" (or call/text me directly)
> 2. I'll send the contract for e-signature
> 3. Upon signing and deposit, we'll schedule your 90-minute kickoff
>
> If you have questions, I'm available anytime. And if you'd prefer to walk through this proposal together on a quick call, just say the word.
>
> Looking forward to building this with you.

## Proposal Delivery: Present It Live

**Best practice:** Walk through the proposal live on Zoom. Don't just email it and wait. Here's why:

- **You control the narrative.** When they read it alone, they interpret. When you present it, you guide their understanding.
- **You handle objections in real time.** A concern that festers for 3 days in email gets resolved in 30 seconds on a call.
- **You close faster.** Live presentations convert at 2-3x the rate of emailed proposals.

### The Live Proposal Walkthrough (15-20 Minutes)

> "Hey [Name], I've put together a proposal based on everything we discussed. Let me walk you through it — shouldn't take more than 15 minutes — and then we can talk through any questions."

Walk through each section. Pause after the Investment section. Ask:

> "Does the investment make sense given what we laid out?"

Their answer tells you everything. If they say yes, close. If they hesitate, dig in: "What's on your mind?"

### The Loom Video Alternative

If a live walkthrough isn't possible (scheduling, timezone, etc.), record a 3-5 minute Loom video walking through the proposal. This is significantly better than a cold email with a PDF attached.

**Loom video structure:**
1. Quick personal intro (15 seconds): "Hey [Name], I recorded this quick walkthrough so you can see the proposal and hear my thinking."
2. Walk through each section on screen (3-4 minutes)
3. Close with CTA (15 seconds): "Let me know if you have any questions. If it looks good, just reply 'let's go' and I'll send the contract."

The personal touch of video — your face, your voice, your enthusiasm — makes the proposal feel human. An emailed PDF feels transactional.

## Proposal Timing: Speed Matters

| Scenario | Send Proposal By |
|----------|-----------------|
| Strong discovery + presentation | Within 24 hours |
| Good discovery, needs follow-up info | Within 48 hours |
| Multiple decision makers (need second call) | Within 24 hours of final call |

**Why speed matters:** The prospect's pain is freshest right after the presentation. Every day you wait, the urgency fades. They get pulled back into daily operations. They start second-guessing. Other priorities creep in.

Send it while the conversation is still ringing in their ears.

## Follow-Up Cadence After Sending the Proposal

Once the proposal is sent, you're in follow-up mode. Here's the cadence:

| Day | Action | Message |
|-----|--------|---------|
| Day 0 | Send proposal | Proposal email with Loom video or live walkthrough |
| Day 3 | Check-in | "Hey [Name], just checking in — did the proposal land okay? Happy to jump on a quick call if anything needs clarification." |
| Day 7 | Value-add | Share a relevant case study, insight, or data point. "Thought of you when I saw this — [link/insight]. This is exactly the kind of result we'd target for [Business Name]." |
| Day 14 | Final follow-up | "Hey [Name], last follow-up from me on this. The proposal and offer stand whenever you're ready. If the timing isn't right, no worries at all — I'll check back in 90 days. Wishing you a great [month/quarter]." |
| Day 30 | Move to nurture | Update CRM status. Set 90-day reminder. Continue LinkedIn engagement. |

### The Rule of Three

**Never follow up more than 3 times on a proposal.** Three is professional. Four is pushy. Five is desperate.

After 3 follow-ups without a response, the prospect has made their decision — they just haven't told you. Respect it. Move on. Check back in 90 days with a fresh angle.

### What If They Ghost?

People ghost. It's not personal. It's capacity.

Common reasons prospects go silent:
- They got busy and it fell off their radar
- They had an internal budget conversation that didn't go well
- They're comparing you to another option
- The timing genuinely isn't right
- They feel bad saying no directly

None of these mean your discovery was bad or your proposal was wrong. It means life happened.

The best response to ghosting: leave the door open with warmth and move on.

> "Hey [Name], no worries at all if the timing isn't right. The door's always open. I'll keep an eye on [Business Name] and if I see something that could help, I'll send it your way. Wishing you the best."

This message does three things:
1. Removes pressure (they don't feel hunted)
2. Shows confidence (you're not desperate)
3. Keeps the relationship alive (they'll remember your professionalism when timing changes)

## Closing Psychology

### The Power of "What Would Make This a Yes?"

If a prospect says no or "not right now," there's one question you must always ask:

> "I appreciate you being straight with me. Can I ask — what would have made this a yes?"

This is gold. Their answer tells you either:
- What you could improve in your process
- What's genuinely blocking them (budget, timing, partner, fear)
- That it's a "not now" instead of a "never" — and you can set a specific revisit date

Never end a lost deal without asking this question.

### The "Not Now" Is Not a "No"

When someone says "the timing isn't right," respect it — but lock in a future conversation.

> "Totally get it. Would it make sense to reconnect in [90 days / after busy season / in Q3]? I'll set a reminder on my end."

Then actually follow up when you said you would. The prospects who close 6 months later are often the best clients — because they had time to think, and they came back because the need was real.

### Handling "I Need to Talk to My Partner/Spouse"

This is one of the most common stalls. Handle it preemptively:

**In discovery (Module 3.4):** "Besides yourself, who else would be involved in a decision like this?" If there's a partner, get them on the presentation call.

**If it comes up at proposal stage:** "Completely understand. Would it help if I jumped on a quick 15-minute call with both of you? That way [partner name] can get answers directly instead of secondhand."

Never let the prospect become the salesperson for you. They'll forget 70% of what you said, misquote the pricing, and fail to convey the value. Get in the room with the decision maker.

## When They Say Yes

This is the best part. A "yes" triggers a specific sequence. Don't wing it.

### Step 1: Send the Contract (Same Day)

Use a professional e-signature platform (DocuSign, PandaDoc, or similar). The contract should include:
- Scope of the Architecture phase
- Timeline
- Investment ($15,000) and payment schedule (50/50)
- What's included and what's not
- Cancellation terms
- Signatures for both parties

### Step 2: Collect 50% Deposit ($7,500)

Send the Stripe payment link immediately upon contract signing. Don't wait for them to ask how to pay. Remove friction.

> "Awesome — the contract is signed. Here's the link for the initial deposit. Once that's in, we'll schedule your kickoff within the week."

### Step 3: Create Client Folder

Set up the client folder from the agency template. This includes:
- Client info sheet
- Discovery notes
- Proposal (signed)
- Kickoff prep materials
- Content brief template
- Reporting template

### Step 4: Schedule Week 1 Kickoff (90 Minutes)

Send the calendar invite for the kickoff call. Include:
- Zoom link
- Pre-kickoff questionnaire (they fill this out before the call)
- Who should attend (owner + any key team members)

> "I've scheduled our kickoff for [date] at [time]. Before the call, please fill out the attached questionnaire — it helps us hit the ground running. If there's anyone else on your team who should be in the room, feel free to invite them."

### Step 5: Send Welcome Email

Within 24 hours of contract signing, send a welcome email:

> Subject: Welcome to the AI OS Partner Program, [Name]

> 3. **Access** — We'll need admin access to [list: Google Business Profile, social accounts, website CMS, etc.]. I'll send detailed instructions separately.

## Proposal and Close Checklist

Use this checklist for every proposal:

### Pre-Proposal
- [ ] Discovery notes reviewed and organized
- [ ] Presentation completed with positive signals
- [ ] Qualification score 3+ out of 7
- [ ] Decision maker(s) identified and engaged
- [ ] Specific next step agreed on during presentation

### Proposal Creation
- [ ] Section 1: Their situation (in their words, with their numbers)
- [ ] Section 2: The opportunity (specific to their business)
- [ ] Section 3: Architecture phase details
- [ ] Section 4: Investment with payment structure
- [ ] Section 5: Week-by-week timeline
- [ ] Section 6: Clear next step ("Reply 'let's go'")
- [ ] Proofread: business name, owner name, numbers all correct
- [ ] Loom video recorded (if not presenting live)

### Post-Proposal
- [ ] Proposal delivered within 48 hours of presentation
- [ ] Day 3 follow-up sent
- [ ] Day 7 value-add sent
- [ ] Day 14 final follow-up sent (if needed)
- [ ] CRM updated with proposal status
- [ ] If won: contract, payment, folder, kickoff, welcome email, tracking — all completed

## Real-World Example: The Proposal That Closed Fox Valley

The Fox Valley Plumbing & Backflow proposal was effective because it reflected exactly what the owner told us during discovery:

- **Their situation:** Great reputation, 50+ reviews, BBB A+, but zero systems for content, lead follow-up, or operations. Owner was doing everything manually.
- **The opportunity:** Automated review requests alone could double their review count in months. Content pipeline would establish them as the go-to plumber in Elgin. Lead response automation would capture business their competitors were getting.
- **The investment:** $15K Architecture, with clear ROI math: even 2-3 additional jobs per month from better lead response would cover the investment.
- **The timeline:** 6 weeks to blueprint, 8 weeks to install.

The proposal was 4 pages. Not 20. Not 50. Four pages that were 100% specific to their business.

Today: 125+ Google reviews. Full content pipeline. $8,500/month ongoing client. The proposal worked because it was a mirror of their reality, not a catalog of our capabilities.

## Key Takeaway

The proposal converts when it reflects the prospect's world back to them — their pain, their numbers, their words, their goals. Send it fast (within 48 hours), present it live whenever possible, and follow up exactly 3 times. When they say yes, execute the onboarding sequence immediately and professionally. When they say no, ask "what would have made this a yes?" and learn from every answer. The close is not a moment — it's the natural result of a great discovery, a clear presentation, and a proposal that makes the next step obvious.

## What to Practice Today

1. **Write a practice proposal** for a hypothetical prospect — a restaurant owner with 2 locations, spending 25 hours/week on admin, losing leads to slow follow-up. Use the 6-section template.
2. **Record a 3-minute Loom video** walking through your practice proposal. Watch it back: do you sound confident? Do you reference their specific situation? Is the CTA clear?
3. **Practice the "What would make this a yes?" question** out loud 5 times. Get comfortable with whatever comes back.
4. **Walk through the "When They Say Yes" sequence** — all 6 steps. Know it cold so that when the moment comes, the experience is seamless.
