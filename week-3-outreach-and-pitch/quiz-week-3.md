## Section 1: Cold Calling and Outreach (Questions 1-5)

### Question 1

What is the best opening line for a cold call to a business owner?

A) "Hi, my name is Jake from Nichefinders AI. We provide AI operating systems for small businesses."
B) "Hey [Name], this is Jake with Nichefinders AI — I know I'm catching you out of the blue, is this a bad time?"
C) "Good morning! Do you have a few minutes to hear about how AI can transform your business?"
D) "Hey [Name], I've been looking at your business and I think you need our help."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Why this matters:** The pattern interrupt ("catching you out of the blue") acknowledges reality, which is disarming. "Is this a bad time?" uses the Chris Voss "no" technique — when they say "no, what's up," they've given you micro-permission to continue while feeling in control. Options A and C trigger immediate "this is a sales call" recognition. Option D is presumptuous and puts them on the defensive.

---

### Question 2

How many voicemails should you leave in a single outreach cadence?

A) 1
B) 2
C) 3
D) As many as needed until they respond

**Correct Answer: B**

**Why this matters:** Two voicemails is the limit. The first plants a seed. The second shows persistence. A third starts to feel intrusive. More than that damages the brand and burns the bridge. Your outreach cadence includes other channels (email, LinkedIn) to reinforce the message without over-calling.

### Question 3

A gatekeeper asks "What is this regarding?" What is the best response?

A) "It's a personal matter."
B) "I'm returning his call."
C) "We help businesses like yours automate operations with AI. I just wanted to see if the owner would be open to a quick conversation."
D) "I'd rather discuss that directly with the owner."

**Correct Answer: C**

**Why this matters:** Honesty wins with gatekeepers. Options A and B are lies that get exposed quickly and destroy credibility. Option D is dismissive and rude — gatekeepers remember that. Option C is truthful, brief, and professional. Treat gatekeepers with respect and they'll help you reach the decision maker.

---

### Question 4

What is the primary goal of a cold call?

A) Close the deal on the phone
B) Book a 45-minute discovery call
C) Deliver the full AI OS pitch
D) Get their email address

**Correct Answer: B**

**Why this matters:** The cold call has exactly one job: book the meeting. You don't close deals on cold calls. You don't deliver full pitches on cold calls. You create enough curiosity that the prospect says yes to 15-20 minutes on their calendar. Everything else happens in discovery.

---

### Question 5

On LinkedIn, how many outreach messages should you send before stopping if there's no reply?

A) 1
B) 3
C) 5
D) Keep going until they respond or block you

**Correct Answer: B**

**Why this matters:** The Rule of Three applies to LinkedIn just like cold calling. Three messages: initial outreach, a value-add bump, and a final polite close. After three unanswered messages, move them to nurture. More than three is perceived as harassment, damages your personal brand, and burns prospects who might have been ready later.

## Section 2: Discovery Call (Questions 6-10)

### Question 6

In the 45-minute discovery call structure, when should you first present the AI OS solution?

A) In the first 5 minutes to set the stage
B) Around minute 15, after initial rapport
C) Around minute 35, after deep discovery and qualification
D) Only in the follow-up presentation call, never on discovery

**Correct Answer: C**

**Why this matters:** You earn the right to present by listening first. The first 30+ minutes are for understanding their business, uncovering pain, and qualifying. If you pitch at minute 5 or 15, you're prescribing before diagnosing. The prospect won't trust your solution if they don't believe you understand their problem. Wait until minute 35 and keep it to 5 minutes.

### Question 7

A prospect says "The scheduling has been an absolute nightmare." Using the Chris Voss mirroring technique, what's the best response?

A) "I totally understand. We can fix that."
B) "Tell me more about your scheduling challenges."
C) "A nightmare?"
D) "That sounds really frustrating. Here's how we handle scheduling..."

**Correct Answer: C**

**Why this matters:** Mirroring is repeating the last 1-3 words with a slight upward inflection. "A nightmare?" invites them to elaborate without directing the conversation. Options A and D jump to solving before fully understanding. Option B is a fine follow-up question but isn't mirroring. The power of mirroring is that it feels natural and validating while drawing out deeper detail.

### Question 8

In the SPIN framework, what is the purpose of Implication questions?

A) To identify their current situation and processes
B) To uncover the specific problems they're facing
C) To help them quantify the true cost of their problems
D) To get them to envision life with the problem solved

**Correct Answer: C**

**Why this matters:** Implication questions transform vague frustrations into concrete financial costs. "Follow-up is messy" becomes "we're losing $15K/month in missed leads." When the prospect quantifies their own pain, the $15K Architecture phase suddenly looks like a bargain. Situation questions (A) map the facts. Problem questions (B) identify issues. Need-payoff questions (D) paint the future. Implication is where the real selling happens.

### Question 9

A discovery call ends and you scored the prospect 2 out of 7 on the qualification scorecard. What should you do?

A) Send the proposal anyway — you never know
B) Schedule a presentation with Corey immediately
C) Decline politely, leave the door open, and set a 90-day check-in
D) Call them back tomorrow and push harder on the pain points

**Correct Answer: C**

**Why this matters:** Not every prospect is a fit, and pretending otherwise wastes everyone's time. A 2/7 means the fundamentals aren't there — decision maker missing, no budget, no timeline, or no clear pain. Sending a proposal (A) or pushing harder (D) won't change the underlying reality. Politely declining (C) preserves the relationship and protects the agency's time for qualified prospects.

### Question 10

What is the ideal talk-to-listen ratio on a discovery call?

A) 50/50 — equal conversation
B) 60% talking, 40% listening — you need to educate them
C) 20% talking, 80% listening — they should do most of the talking
D) 30% talking, 70% listening — a slight edge to listening

**Correct Answer: C**

**Why this matters:** Discovery is about uncovering, not educating. If you're talking 50% or more, you're presenting — not discovering. The 80/20 ratio ensures the prospect does most of the talking, which means: (1) you learn more about their real pain, (2) they feel heard and trusted, and (3) they talk themselves into the solution. The more they talk, the more they trust you.

## Section 3: Presentation and Close (Questions 11-15)

### Question 11

In the presentation, what order should you present costs?

A) Your price first, then alternatives, then cost of doing nothing
B) Cost of doing nothing first, then alternatives, then your price
C) Your price alongside competitors for a fair comparison
D) Just your price — don't mention alternatives

**Correct Answer: B**

**Why this matters:** This is the anchoring principle. When you present the cost of doing nothing ($200K+ annually), followed by hiring ($150K+), followed by other agencies ($36-96K/year), the $15K Architecture phase feels modest by comparison. Presenting your price first (A) creates sticker shock with no context. The sequence creates a psychological frame where your investment seems not just reasonable, but like a bargain.

### Question 12

During the presentation, a prospect says "I need to think about it." What is the best response?

A) "Take your time — no pressure."
B) "Of course. What specifically are you weighing? I want to make sure I've given you everything you need."
C) "I can offer a 10% discount if you decide today."
D) "Sure — I'll follow up next week."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Why this matters:** "I need to think about it" usually means there's a specific concern they haven't voiced. Option B draws it out respectfully. Option A removes all urgency and gives them permission to disappear. Option C devalues the work and sets a dangerous precedent. Option D is too passive — "next week" is vague and gets forgotten. By asking what they're specifically weighing, you can address the real concern in real time.

---

### Question 13

What is the maximum number of times you should follow up on a sent proposal with no response?

A) 2 times
B) 3 times
C) 5 times
D) Until they respond or explicitly say no

**Correct Answer: B**

**Why this matters:** The Rule of Three is consistent across all outreach channels. Three follow-ups after a proposal: Day 3 (check-in), Day 7 (value-add), Day 14 (final close). After three, move to nurture with a 90-day reminder. More than three crosses the line from professional persistence to annoying pressure, and damages the brand.

---

### Question 14

A prospect says "Can you do it for less?" regarding the $15,000 Architecture phase. What is the correct response?

A) "Let me talk to my team and see what we can do."
B) "We can drop it to $12K if you sign this week."
C) "The $15K reflects CEO-level involvement and the depth of the architecture. We don't discount, but the ROI makes it a no-brainer within the first month."
D) "How much were you thinking?"

**Correct Answer: C**

**Why this matters:** Never discount. Option A signals weakness and says the price was inflated. Option B sets a precedent and devalues the work. Option D puts you in a negotiation you shouldn't be in. Option C holds firm with a clear rationale (CEO involvement, depth) and redirects to ROI. If the value is clear, the price is fair. If the value isn't clear, discounting won't fix that — better discovery will.

### Question 15

After a presentation, what must happen before you hang up?

A) Thank them for their time and promise to follow up
B) Send a calendar invite for the next call while still on the line
C) Ask them to email you with any questions
D) Give them a week to think it over

**Correct Answer: B**

**Why this matters:** Never end a call without a committed, calendared next step. Sending the invite while still on the call eliminates the gap where momentum dies. "I'll follow up" (A) is vague. "Email me questions" (C) puts the burden on them. "A week to think" (D) gives uncertainty time to grow. Pin the next step, send the invite, confirm they got it — then hang up.

## Section 4: Sales Psychology Application (Questions 16-20)

### Question 16

You're at a networking event and someone asks "What do you do?" Which response creates the most curiosity?

A) "I'm in AI consulting for small businesses."
B) "We build AI operating systems for businesses doing $1 to $10 million. Think of it as a digital chief of staff that handles your follow-ups, content, and gives you a briefing every morning."
C) "I work at Nichefinders AI. We do marketing and operations automation."
D) "I help companies leverage cutting-edge AI technology to streamline their workflow optimization and enhance operational synergy."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Why this matters:** Option B creates a mental image (digital chief of staff), includes specificity ($1-10M), and uses concrete outcomes (follow-ups, content, briefing). Option A is forgettable — sounds like everyone else. Option C is a label, not a pitch. Option D is jargon-laden nonsense that makes people's eyes glaze over. The best pitches are specific, visual, and jargon-free.

### Question 17

A prospect says "We already use ChatGPT." What is the most effective reframe?

A) "ChatGPT is great, but our system is better."
B) "ChatGPT is a tool — the AI OS is a system. ChatGPT doesn't know your business. The AI OS already knows your team, your numbers, and your strategy. It's the difference between having a calculator and having an accountant."
C) "ChatGPT can't do what we do."
D) "Most of our clients started with ChatGPT before realizing they needed something more professional."

**Correct Answer: B**

**Why this matters:** The calculator vs. accountant analogy is memorable, accurate, and non-threatening. It doesn't trash ChatGPT (A and C both do, which makes the prospect feel judged for their choice). Option D subtly implies they're unprofessional. Option B validates their use of ChatGPT while clearly differentiating the AI OS through a relatable analogy.

### Question 18

Why does asking "Is this a bad time?" work better than "Do you have a minute?" on a cold call?

A) It's more polite
B) It invites a "no" response, which gives the prospect control and lowers their guard
C) It takes less time to say
D) It sounds more professional

**Correct Answer: B**

**Why this matters:** This is the Chris Voss "no" technique. When people say "no" ("No, it's not a bad time"), they feel in control of the conversation. That feeling of control lowers their defensive instincts. "Do you have a minute?" invites a "no" that ends the call ("No, I'm busy"). The psychology of how the "no" lands is what makes this work — it's not about politeness or professionalism, it's about triggering a psychological response that opens the conversation.

### Question 19

During a discovery call, you ask "What would it mean for you personally if you got 20 hours a week back?" The prospect pauses for 8 seconds without responding. What should you do?

A) Rephrase the question to make it easier to answer
B) Offer some examples: "Maybe more time with family, or a vacation?"
C) Wait in silence — they're processing a deep question
D) Move on to the next question to keep momentum

**Correct Answer: C**

**Why this matters:** Strategic silence is one of the most powerful tools in discovery. An 8-second pause after a deep question means the prospect is thinking seriously — which means you asked a great question. Rephrasing (A) steals the moment. Offering examples (B) puts words in their mouth. Moving on (D) wastes the question. The discomfort of silence is yours to bear, not theirs. Wait. Their answer will be worth it.

### Question 20

A prospect who said "not now" three months ago just had a busy season end. You're checking back in. What is the best approach?

A) Send the original proposal again with a note saying "Ready when you are"
B) Call and say "Remember me? I'm following up on our conversation from January"
C) Reach out with a new angle — a relevant insight, case study, or changed circumstance — and offer a fresh conversation
D) Send a LinkedIn connection request as if you've never spoken

**Correct Answer: C**

**Why this matters:** Three months is a long time. Their situation has changed. Resending the old proposal (A) feels stale and lazy. A generic "remember me?" (B) is awkward. Pretending you haven't spoken (D) is dishonest. Option C respects the time gap by bringing something new — a case study from a similar business, an industry insight, or a reference to their busy season ending. It shows you've been paying attention and gives them a reason to re-engage without feeling pressured.
