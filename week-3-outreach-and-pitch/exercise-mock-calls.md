# Week 3 Exercise — Mock Call Role-Play Scenarios

**Estimated Time:** 60-90 minutes (15-20 minutes per scenario)
**Format:** Partner role-play (one person plays the prospect, one plays the salesperson, then switch)
**Passing Score:** 16/20 across all rubric categories

---

## Instructions

These four scenarios simulate real discovery and pitch conversations you'll encounter at Nichefinder AI Agency. Each scenario includes a detailed prospect persona, their mindset, pain points, likely objections, and the ideal outcome.

**How to run each scenario:**

1. **Reader (prospect)** reads the full persona card silently. Get into character — think about what this person cares about, what annoys them, and how they'd talk.
2. **Caller (salesperson)** gets only the "What You Know Going In" section. You do NOT see the full persona card.
3. **Run the call** for 10-15 minutes. Cover: opening, rapport, discovery (SPIN questions), brief solution mention, and next step.
4. **Debrief** for 5 minutes. The prospect gives honest feedback. Score using the rubric.
5. **Switch roles** and repeat with a different scenario.

## Scenario 1: The Overwhelmed Restaurant Owner

### Prospect Persona Card (For the person playing the prospect)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Name** | Maria Gonzalez |
| **Age** | 45 |
| **Business** | Casa Maria Mexican Restaurant |
| **Locations** | 2 (original in Tampa, second opened 8 months ago in St. Pete) |
| **Revenue** | ~$2.2M combined |
| **Employees** | 28 (14 each location) |
| **Years in Business** | 11 |
| **Role** | Owner/Operator — she's at one location every day, tries to manage both remotely |

**Her Personality:**
- Warm, talkative, trusts people quickly but has been burned before
- Works 65-70 hours/week and is proud of it (but secretly exhausted)
- Not tech-savvy — uses her phone for everything, no laptop
- Makes decisions based on gut feeling, not data
- Loyal to her current vendors even when they underperform
- Speaks with passion about her food and her staff

**Her Pain Points:**
- Spending 15+ hours/week on scheduling staff across 2 locations (uses text messages and a whiteboard)
- Inventory tracking is a mess — she over-orders because she can't see what both locations have
- Social media died 3 months ago when her niece (who was doing it for free) went back to college
- Yelp reviews are mixed (3.8 stars) because no one responds to them or asks happy customers to review
- The St. Pete location is underperforming — she thinks it's a food issue, but it's actually a visibility issue (poor Google presence)
- Tried Yelp advertising ($800/month for 6 months). It didn't work. She's skeptical of "marketing people."

**Her Objections (will bring these up naturally):**
1. "I don't really understand AI — is this going to be complicated?"
2. "We spent $5K on Yelp ads and got nothing. How is this different?"
3. "$15K is a lot of money for a restaurant. My margins are thin."
4. "I'd need to talk to my husband about this. He handles the money."

**Her Hidden Motivation (she won't say this unless you ask the right questions):**
She wants to spend more time with her two teenage daughters. She hasn't had a real day off in 8 months. She opened the second location to build generational wealth, but it's consuming her life instead of creating freedom.

**Ideal Outcome:**
She agrees to a discovery call with Corey, focused on how the AI OS could handle scheduling, content, reviews, and inventory visibility across both locations. She understands this isn't "marketing" — it's an operating system.

---

### What You Know Going In (For the salesperson)

- **Business:** Casa Maria Mexican Restaurant, Tampa + St. Pete (2 locations)
- **Owner:** Maria Gonzalez
- **Found via:** Google Maps search for restaurants with 2+ locations and inconsistent review profiles
- **Observation:** Tampa location has 180 reviews (4.2 stars). St. Pete has 23 reviews (3.8 stars). Social media last posted 3 months ago. No blog. Website is basic — no online ordering integration visible.
- **Your approach:** Cold call or warm LinkedIn message to book a discovery call

**Conversation Guide:**
- **Open:** Pattern interrupt, acknowledge you're catching her out of the blue. Reference the two locations to show you did research.
- **Discover:** Ask about how she manages both locations. What takes the most time? What fell off when the second location opened? How is content/social/reviews being handled?
- **Listen for:** The personal toll. Restaurant owners are often so focused on the business that they don't realize how much it's costing them personally.
- **Present (briefly):** AI OS as an operational backbone — not marketing. Scheduling, inventory visibility, automated review requests, content on autopilot.
- **Close:** Book a 45-minute call with Corey. Mention he works with businesses her size and has seen this pattern before.

## Scenario 2: The Frustrated HVAC Contractor

### Prospect Persona Card (For the person playing the prospect)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Name** | Dave Mitchell |
| **Age** | 52 |
| **Business** | Mitchell Mechanical (HVAC) |
| **Location** | Charlotte, NC metro |
| **Revenue** | ~$3.2M |
| **Employees** | 18 (12 technicians, 3 office staff, 2 sales reps, 1 dispatcher) |
| **Years in Business** | 22 |
| **Role** | Owner — still goes on calls when shorthanded, handles all major decisions |

**His Personality:**
- Direct, no-nonsense, doesn't like small talk
- Skeptical of anything that sounds like "tech bro" talk
- Respects people who know their stuff and wastes no time on those who don't
- Competitive — mentions his competitors frequently
- Grew the business from a one-truck operation. Proud of that.
- Makes fast decisions when convinced. Slow to convince.

**His Pain Points:**
- Losing 8-12 leads per month to competitors who respond faster (he suspects, doesn't have data to prove it)
- Tried Zapier 18 months ago to automate follow-ups. It broke after 2 weeks. He blames the tool, not the setup.
- His 2 sales reps are manually following up via text message — inconsistent, no tracking
- No content being created. Website hasn't been updated in 2 years.
- Referrals are 60% of business, but he knows he's over-reliant on them
- Office staff spends 10+ hours/week on paperwork that could be digital
- Recently lost a $40K commercial job because the proposal went out 3 days late (competitor responded same-day)

**His Objections (will bring these up naturally):**
1. "We tried automation before. Zapier was a nightmare."
2. "I don't have time to learn new systems. My guys definitely don't."
3. "How do I know you won't just take my money and disappear like the last marketing company?"
4. "My busy season starts in 6 weeks. Can't afford to be distracted."

**His Hidden Motivation (he won't say this unless you ask the right questions):**
He wants to step back from day-to-day operations within 3 years and have his son take over. But the business is too dependent on him personally. If he steps away, things fall apart. He needs systems that run without him.

**Ideal Outcome:**
He agrees to a discovery call with Corey, focused on lead response automation, proposal speed, and building systems that reduce his personal involvement. Frame it as "building a business that runs without you in it."

---

### What You Know Going In (For the salesperson)

- **Business:** Mitchell Mechanical (HVAC), Charlotte NC
- **Owner:** Dave Mitchell
- **Found via:** LinkedIn — he posted about losing a commercial job because "the process is too slow around here"
- **Observation:** 67 Google reviews (4.4 stars), but no response to reviews. Website looks dated — no blog, no service area pages, basic contact form only. Competitors (Morris-Jenkins, ARS) dominate local search.
- **Your approach:** LinkedIn warm outreach (reference his post) to book a discovery call

**Conversation Guide:**
- **Open:** Reference his LinkedIn post about losing the commercial job. Show empathy, not judgment.
- **Discover:** Ask about his lead-to-close process. How do leads come in? Who follows up? How long does it take? What happened with Zapier?
- **Listen for:** The Zapier failure — this is a trust barrier you must address. Also listen for the "I'm the bottleneck" language.
- **Present (briefly):** AI OS is not Zapier. It's a managed system designed and maintained by the agency. He doesn't learn anything — it runs in the background. Reference Fox Valley as a similar contractor who had the same concerns.
- **Close:** Book a 45-minute call with Corey. Dave respects owner-to-owner conversations. Mention Corey runs the business and does every Architecture engagement personally.

## Scenario 3: The Bottlenecked Agency Owner

### Prospect Persona Card (For the person playing the prospect)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Name** | Jordan Park |
| **Age** | 35 |
| **Business** | Parkway Digital (marketing agency) |
| **Location** | Austin, TX |
| **Revenue** | ~$1.8M |
| **Employees** | 8 (3 account managers, 2 designers, 1 developer, 1 content writer, 1 strategist) |
| **Years in Business** | 5 |
| **Role** | Founder/CEO — reviews every deliverable, attends every client call, writes all proposals |

**His Personality:**
- Smart, fast-talking, always multitasking
- Knows about AI and has opinions — uses ChatGPT daily for content drafts
- Competitive with other agencies — follows what they do
- Has shiny object syndrome — jumps on new tools every few months
- Gets excited easily but doesn't follow through (started 3 automations, finished zero)
- Genuinely cares about his team but burns them out with his pace

**His Pain Points:**
- Personally involved in every client deliverable — 50+ hours/week on production, not strategy
- Content writer can produce 4 blog posts/month. Clients want 8-12.
- Client reporting takes 6-8 hours/month per client (15 clients). That's 90+ hours/month on reports alone.
- Pitched 6 new clients last quarter, won 2. Close rate dropping because proposals take too long.
- Team morale is slipping — his best designer is interviewing elsewhere
- Uses ChatGPT for drafts but spends hours editing because "it doesn't sound right"
- Has tried Jasper, Copy.ai, and Writesonic. None stuck.

**His Objections (will bring these up naturally):**
1. "I already use ChatGPT every day. What would this do that ChatGPT doesn't?"
2. "We're an agency — we should be able to do this ourselves. It feels weird hiring another agency."
3. "8 employees is small. Are we big enough for this?"
4. "I'm not sure I can justify $15K to my accountant when we're already tight on margins."

**His Hidden Motivation (he won't say this unless you ask the right questions):**
He started the agency to have freedom and creative control. Now he's more trapped than when he had a corporate job. He hasn't taken a vacation in 2 years. His girlfriend is starting to resent how much he works. He needs to go from doing the work to running the business.

**Ideal Outcome:**
He agrees to a discovery call with Corey, focused on how the AI OS can multiply his content output (4 posts to 36+), automate client reporting, and free him from daily production work so he can focus on strategy and new business.

---

### What You Know Going In (For the salesperson)

- **Business:** Parkway Digital (marketing agency), Austin TX
- **Owner:** Jordan Park
- **Found via:** LinkedIn — he posts frequently about agency life, scaling challenges, and AI tools
- **Observation:** His agency website shows 15 active clients. Team page has 8 people. He posts 4-5x/week on LinkedIn about being overwhelmed (half-joking). Recent post: "If anyone has figured out how to clone themselves, DM me." His content quality is good but his agency's own blog hasn't been updated in 2 months.
- **Your approach:** LinkedIn warm outreach (engage with his posts for a week first, then DM)

**Conversation Guide:**
- **Open:** Reference his "clone yourself" post. Light, empathetic. "I can't clone you, but I might have the next best thing."
- **Discover:** Ask about his daily routine. What does he do that no one else can? What would he delegate if he could? How is content production working? How long do reports take?
- **Listen for:** The "I'm the bottleneck" admission. Also the ChatGPT experience — he's sophisticated enough to know AI but hasn't found the right system.
- **Present (briefly):** The calculator vs. accountant analogy. ChatGPT is a tool he goes to. The AI OS is a system that runs for him. Specifically: automated client reports, content pipeline that produces 9+ pieces/week, and proposal templates that cut turnaround in half.
- **Close:** Book a 45-minute call with Corey. Jordan respects founders. Position Corey as a fellow agency owner who solved the same problem.

## Scenario 4: The Overwhelmed Nonprofit Director

### Prospect Persona Card (For the person playing the prospect)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Name** | Patricia "Trish" Williams |
| **Age** | 48 |
| **Business** | Hope Horizon Foundation (youth mentorship nonprofit) |
| **Location** | Atlanta, GA |
| **Revenue** | ~$1.6M annual (grants + donations + events) |
| **Employees** | 20 (12 program staff, 4 admin, 2 fundraising, 1 comms, 1 ED assistant) |
| **Years in Business** | 9 |
| **Role** | Executive Director — manages everything from board relations to daily ops |

**Her Personality:**
- Mission-driven, passionate, articulate
- Speaks in stories — every answer includes a personal anecdote about a kid they've helped
- Cautious with money — accountable to a board and donors, every dollar must be justified
- Open to technology but has been disappointed by past implementations (CRM migration that took 8 months)
- Collaborative decision maker — won't commit without board input
- Deeply skeptical of anything that sounds "corporate" or "salesy"

**Her Pain Points:**
- Donor management is split across 3 systems: an old Salesforce instance, Excel spreadsheets, and physical files. Nobody trusts any single source.
- Grant reporting takes 3 full weeks per quarter — pulling data from multiple places, formatting, writing narratives
- Annual fundraising gala (their biggest event, ~$400K) is planned on sticky notes and email chains
- Social media is handled by an intern who rotates every 6 months — no continuity, no strategy
- Board wants a "digital transformation" but hasn't allocated specific budget for it
- Staff turnover is high (30%/year) because admin burden keeps people from doing the program work they signed up for

**Her Objections (will bring these up naturally):**
1. "We're a nonprofit — $15K is a lot of money for us. That's almost a full-time staff salary."
2. "I'd need board approval for something like this. I can't decide alone."
3. "We tried migrating to a new CRM two years ago. It was a disaster. I'm nervous about any big system change."
4. "Our funders want to see money going to programs, not technology."

**Her Hidden Motivation (she won't say this unless you ask the right questions):**
She's thinking about retiring in 3-4 years. She wants to leave the organization in a position where it can run without her. Right now, she IS the organization — all the institutional knowledge, all the donor relationships, all the systems live in her head. If she left tomorrow, it would take years to recover. She needs systems so the mission survives beyond her.

**Ideal Outcome:**
She agrees to a discovery call with Corey, with the understanding that (1) the board will need to be involved before any commitment, and (2) the focus is on reducing admin burden so staff can focus on programs. Frame the AI OS as a way to serve more kids with the same resources — mission-aligned language.

---

### What You Know Going In (For the salesperson)

- **Business:** Hope Horizon Foundation (youth mentorship), Atlanta GA
- **Owner/ED:** Patricia Williams
- **Found via:** Referral from a board member you met at a networking event
- **Observation:** Their website is decent but blog hasn't been updated in 4 months. Event pages are outdated. Social media is sporadic — posts every 2-3 weeks. Their GuideStar profile shows $1.6M revenue. They have a large volunteer base (200+ per their website). The annual gala is mentioned prominently — seems like their flagship event.
- **Your approach:** Warm call — the board member made an introduction via email

**Conversation Guide:**
- **Open:** Reference the board member's introduction by name. Express genuine interest in the mission. Ask about the kids they serve before asking about operations.
- **Discover:** Ask about the daily reality of running the foundation. Where does admin eat into program time? How is data managed? What takes the longest every month?
- **Listen for:** The mission-operations tension. She wants to talk about impact, but the conversation keeps coming back to admin, systems, and data. That tension IS the pain.
- **Present (briefly):** The AI OS as a way to amplify mission impact — not as a tech project. "What if your grant reports wrote themselves from live data? What if donor follow-ups happened automatically? What if your team spent 50% less time on admin and 50% more time with the kids?"
- **Close:** Book a discovery call with Corey. Acknowledge the board requirement proactively: "I completely understand the board needs to be part of this. Let's start with a conversation between you and Corey, and if it makes sense, we'll prepare a brief for the board."

## Grading Rubric

After each scenario, the person who played the prospect scores the salesperson on four dimensions:

### 1. Rapport (1-5 points)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | No rapport built. Felt like a transactional call. |
| 2 | Minimal rapport. A few friendly moments but mostly mechanical. |
| 3 | Decent rapport. Felt comfortable but not deeply connected. |
| 4 | Strong rapport. Felt like the salesperson genuinely cared about my situation. |
| 5 | Exceptional rapport. I'd want to take this call again. Felt heard and valued as a person. |

**What to evaluate:** Did they ask about me as a person, not just my business? Did they use my name naturally? Did they match my energy and communication style? Did they show genuine interest in the mission/work?

### 2. Discovery (1-5 points)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Asked surface-level questions or jumped straight to pitching. |
| 2 | Asked some discovery questions but missed major pain points. |
| 3 | Solid discovery — uncovered 2-3 pain points with decent depth. |
| 4 | Strong discovery — used SPIN effectively, uncovered pain AND implications. |
| 5 | Masterful — uncovered the hidden motivation, quantified pain, made me realize the depth of my own problems. |

**What to evaluate:** Did they use SPIN questions? Did they dig deeper when I mentioned a pain point (vs. moving on)? Did they get to the personal motivation? Did they listen more than they talked?

### 3. Solution Fit (1-5 points)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Presented a generic pitch that didn't connect to anything I said. |
| 2 | Mentioned the AI OS but didn't tie it to my specific pain points. |
| 3 | Connected the solution to 1-2 of my pain points. Somewhat relevant. |
| 4 | Tailored the solution to my specific situation. Used my words and referenced my problems. |
| 5 | Made me feel like the AI OS was designed for my exact business. Connected every pain point to a specific outcome. Used analogies I'd understand. |

**What to evaluate:** Did they use my language, not their jargon? Did they lead with outcomes, not features? Did they reference a relevant client example? Did they address my industry-specific concerns?

### 4. Objection Handling (1-5 points)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Ignored my objections or got defensive. |
| 2 | Acknowledged objections but didn't address them effectively. |
| 3 | Handled 1-2 objections reasonably well. Stumbled on others. |
| 4 | Handled all objections with confidence and empathy. Redirected without dismissing. |
| 5 | Turned objections into opportunities. Made me feel more confident after raising concerns, not less. |

**What to evaluate:** Did they acknowledge my concern before responding? Did they stay calm and confident? Did they avoid discounting or making promises they can't keep? Did they ask follow-up questions to understand the root of my objection?

### Total Score

| Total | Result |
|-------|--------|
| 20/20 | Elite — ready for live calls |
| 18-19 | Strong — minor refinements needed |
| 16-17 | Passing — solid foundation, keep practicing |
| 13-15 | Below passing — review weak areas, re-do the scenario |
| Below 13 | Re-study Modules 3.1-3.6 before attempting again |

## Post-Exercise Reflection

After completing all four scenarios, answer these questions in writing:

1. **Which scenario was hardest for you? Why?**
2. **Which SPIN question type (Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-Payoff) do you need to practice most?**
3. **What objection tripped you up? How would you handle it differently next time?**
4. **Did you uncover the hidden motivation in any scenario? If not, what question would you ask to get there?**
5. **Rate your talk-to-listen ratio honestly. Were you at 20/80 or closer to 50/50?**

Write your answers down. Share them with your manager or training partner. These reflections are where the real improvement happens.
