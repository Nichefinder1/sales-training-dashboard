# Module 3.5 — The Presentation

**Reading Time:** 20 minutes
**Objective:** Learn how to deliver a solution presentation that connects discovery pain to AI OS outcomes, demonstrates real value through live demos, and positions the Architecture phase as the logical next step.

---

## The Presentation Is Not a Pitch — It's a Prescription

By the time you get to the presentation, you've already done the hard work. The discovery call uncovered their pain, their goals, and their buying capability. Now your job is to connect the dots.

Think of it this way: a doctor doesn't walk into the exam room and start prescribing medicine before hearing your symptoms. They listen first. They diagnose. And then — only then — do they prescribe.

Your discovery call was the examination. The presentation is the prescription. And prescriptions work because they're specific to the patient.

This is Corey's call. He presents the solution. Your role is to prepare him with everything from your discovery notes — the pain points, the exact words the prospect used, their goals, their qualification score, and any landmines (objections they raised, past bad experiences, budget concerns). The better your notes, the better his close rate.

That said, you need to understand the presentation structure cold. You'll sit in on these calls. You'll take notes. And eventually, you may run them yourself for qualified prospects. So learn this like it's your job — because it is.

## Before the Presentation: Setting the Stage

### The 24-Hour Prep

Between the discovery call and the presentation, several things need to happen:

| Task | Who | Timing |
|------|-----|--------|
| Send discovery debrief to Corey | You | Within 2 hours of discovery call |
| Send prospect a summary email | You | Same day as discovery call |
| Research 1-2 additional data points | You | Day before presentation |
| Prepare demo environment | You/Team | Day before presentation |
| Review prospect's application (if applicable) | Corey | 30 min before call |
| Confirm the meeting | You | Morning of presentation |

### The Confirmation Message

Send this the morning of the presentation:

> "Hey [Name], looking forward to our call at [time] today. Corey's reviewed everything from our conversation and he's got some ideas specific to [Business Name]. See you at [time]. Here's the Zoom link: [link]"

This does two things: reduces no-shows and primes them to expect something tailored (not generic).

## The 30-Minute Presentation Structure

The presentation is 30 minutes. Tight, focused, and structured. Every minute earns its place.

### Minute 0-2: Opening Reconnection

Corey opens by referencing the discovery call. This shows continuity and proves the agency was listening.

> "Hey [Name], thanks for coming back for round two. [Your Name] gave me a full download from your conversation, and I have to say — [Business Name] is doing some really impressive things. Before I show you anything, I want to make sure I have the picture right.
>
> Last time, you mentioned three things that stood out: [pain 1 in their words], [pain 2 in their words], and [pain 3 in their words]. Is that still accurate, or has anything changed since we talked?"

**Why this matters:**

- **Continuity** — they know the team communicates and takes this seriously
- **Their words** — hearing their own pain reflected back makes it more real
- **Permission to proceed** — "Is that still accurate?" gives them a chance to course-correct, which builds trust
- **Change check** — sometimes priorities shift between calls. You need to know.

If they correct something or add a new pain point, adapt on the fly. The presentation is a framework, not a script.

---

### Minute 2-12: Solution Presentation

This is the core of the presentation. You're connecting their pain to the AI OS — but through outcomes, not features.

#### The Golden Rule: Pain First, Solution Second

For every point you make, follow this structure:

1. **Restate their pain** (in their words)
2. **Show the solution** (what changes)
3. **Prove it works** (with a real example)

**Example flow:**

> "You mentioned you're losing leads because follow-ups don't go out fast enough. Here's what happens in the AI OS: the moment a lead comes in — from your website, Google, a phone call — the system sends an automated response within 60 seconds. Text, email, or both. It follows up on a schedule, and nothing falls through the cracks. Your team just handles the ones that respond.
>
> We built this for a plumbing company in Illinois — Fox Valley Plumbing & Backflow. Before the system, they were manually chasing every lead. Now it's fully automated. Their review count went from about 50 to over 125 because the system also automates review requests after every completed job."

#### What NOT to Do

**Don't list features.** "We use n8n for workflow automation, Airtable for CRM, Claude for AI content, and a Next.js client portal" means nothing to a business owner. It sounds like a tech spec, not a solution.

**Do list outcomes.** "Your follow-ups run automatically, your content publishes itself, your data lives in one dashboard, and you get a briefing every morning telling you exactly where everything stands."

#### The Three Demo Moments

During the solution section, show — don't tell. Three brief demonstrations make the AI OS tangible:

**Demo 1: Client Portal (2 minutes)**

Pull up the All South Lightning Protection client portal (or a demo version). Show:
- Live data: keyword rankings, content metrics, activity log
- How information is organized and accessible
- The "at a glance" experience — everything in one place

> "This is what your dashboard looks like when the system is running. Everything your business is doing online — rankings, content, leads, activity — all in one view. No logging into 5 different tools. No asking your team for updates. It's all here, live."

**Demo 2: Content Pipeline (2 minutes)**

Show the n8n content workflow or a content calendar output. Demonstrate:
- Automated content creation flow
- Output: 9 pieces of content per week (blog + social)
- Content quality: written in the client's voice, industry-specific

> "This is the content engine. Once it's built, it produces 9 pieces of content per week — blog posts, social media, all of it — without anyone on your team writing a word. It knows your business, your voice, your audience. And it runs every single week."

**Demo 3: The System View (2 minutes)**

Show the Airtable backend or a system architecture diagram. Demonstrate:
- How everything connects: leads, content, operations, reporting
- The "operating system" concept — not a collection of tools, but an integrated system
- Where AI handles the work vs. where humans make decisions

> "This is the backbone. Everything feeds into one system. Your leads, your content, your operations, your metrics. The AI handles the execution. Your team handles the decisions. That's what an operating system looks like."

### Minute 12-14: The Transition

After the demos, create a clear transition from "this is what exists" to "this is what it could look like for you."

> "This is what the AI OS looks like when it's fully running — for a business similar to yours. The Architecture phase is where we design YOUR version of this. We take everything you told us about [Business Name], map out the entire system, and show you exactly what it'll look like before a single thing gets built.
>
> Let me walk you through the investment."

This transition is critical because it:
- Anchors what they just saw as achievable for their business
- Positions Architecture as the natural next step
- Creates anticipation for the investment conversation (which is less scary when it follows a compelling demo)

---

### Minute 14-20: Value Analysis / ROI Framing

This is where you present the investment — but you never present price in a vacuum. You always present it against the cost of alternatives and the cost of doing nothing.

#### Step 1: Cost of Doing Nothing

> "Let's look at what it's costing you right now to keep things the way they are.
>
> You mentioned you're personally spending about 20 hours a week on admin and follow-ups. If we value your time at even $100/hour — which is probably conservative for an owner — that's $2,000 a week. Over a year, that's over $100,000 in owner time spent on work that doesn't directly grow the business.
>
> You also mentioned losing roughly 5-10 leads a month due to slow follow-up. If your average job is worth $3,000, and you're losing even 5 per month, that's $15,000/month in missed revenue. Over a year: $180,000.
>
> So conservatively, the current situation is costing you somewhere between $200K-$280K per year in time and lost business. And that doesn't include the opportunity cost — what you COULD be doing with those 20 hours if you weren't buried in admin."

**Use their numbers whenever possible.** If they told you their average job value, their hourly rate, or their lead count during discovery — use those exact figures. Their numbers are more believable than your estimates.

#### Step 2: Cost of Alternatives

| Alternative | Cost | Limitations |
|------------|------|-------------|
| Hire an operations person | $55-75K/year + benefits + training + turnover risk | Still manual, limited to one person's output |
| Hire a marketing team (2-3 people) | $150-250K/year | High overhead, slow ramp-up, management required |
| Traditional marketing agency | $3-8K/month ($36-96K/year) | Generic, no AI, no operations, campaigns end |
| DIY with ChatGPT + free tools | Your time (20+ hrs/week) | No integration, no automation, starts over every time |
| Do nothing | $200-280K/year in lost time + revenue | Problem gets worse as you grow |

#### Step 3: The AI OS Investment

> "The Architecture phase is a one-time investment of $15,000. In that phase, Corey personally designs the entire AI operating system for [Business Name]. You see the full blueprint — every workflow, every automation, every data connection — before deciding whether to move to Installation.
>
> Installation typically runs $25,000 to $75,000 depending on scope and complexity. And then there's an optional Management retainer at $2,500 to $5,000 per month where we keep everything running, optimized, and evolving.
>
> Compared to $200K+ in annual losses, or $150K+ to hire a team, the Architecture phase pays for itself within the first 3-4 weeks of the system being live."

#### The Anchoring Principle

**SALES PSYCHOLOGY:** Anchoring is one of the most powerful cognitive biases in pricing. When you present the cost of alternatives first ($200K in lost revenue, $150K for a team, $96K for a traditional agency), the $15K Architecture phase feels modest by comparison. You're not manipulating — you're providing honest context. But the order matters.

Always present costs in this sequence:
1. Cost of doing nothing (highest number)
2. Cost of hiring (second highest)
3. Cost of alternatives (middle)
4. Your investment (lowest)

By the time they hear $15K, they've already processed numbers 5-10x larger. The frame is set.

### Minute 20-25: Handling Questions and Objections

After the investment section, pause. Let them process. They'll either ask questions or sit quietly.

**If they ask questions:** Great. Answer directly and concisely. Common questions:

| Question | Response |
|----------|----------|
| "What exactly do I get in the Architecture phase?" | "A complete blueprint: system design, workflow maps, AI strategy, data architecture, content plan, and a presentation walking you through exactly how it all works. You see the entire system before deciding to build." |
| "How long does Architecture take?" | "4-6 weeks. We move fast because we've done this before and we know what works." |
| "What if I don't move to Installation?" | "Then you have the blueprint. It's yours to keep. Some clients take the Architecture and implement it themselves, or bring us back later. There's no obligation." |
| "Can I see a contract first?" | "Absolutely. I'll have one to you within 24 hours." |
| "I need to think about it." | "Of course. What specifically are you weighing? I want to make sure I've given you everything you need to make a decision." |
| "Can you do it for less?" | "The $15K reflects the CEO-level attention and the depth of the architecture. We don't discount, but I can tell you every client who's gone through it has said it was worth significantly more than what they paid." |

**If they're silent:** Don't rush to fill the silence. They're processing. Wait 5-7 seconds. If they still haven't spoken, try:

> "What's going through your mind right now?"

That question — calm, curious, without pressure — gives them permission to express whatever they're feeling. Maybe it's excitement. Maybe it's hesitation. Either way, you need to know.

### Minute 25-30: The Close

Closing is not about pressure. It's about clarity. You're asking: "Does this make sense for you?" and then making the next step easy.

#### The Assumptive Close

> "I think the Architecture phase is a perfect fit for where [Business Name] is right now. I'll have a formal proposal to you by [specific day]. Does morning or afternoon work better to walk through it together?"

This assumes the answer is yes and moves directly to logistics. It works when the call has gone well and they've been nodding along.

#### The Choice Close

> "Most clients start with the Architecture phase. Some want to move fast and bundle Architecture with Installation. Which approach feels better for [Business Name]?"

This gives them a choice between two yeses — not a yes or no. Either answer moves forward.

#### The "What Would Need to Be True?" Close

Use this when you sense hesitation:

> "I hear you. What would need to be true for this to be a yes?"

This is the most powerful question in sales. It shifts from you persuading them to them telling you exactly what they need to buy. Whatever they say — "I'd need to see a contract," "I'd need my partner on board," "I'd need to know the timeline" — becomes your action item.

#### The Low-Risk Close

If they're nervous about the commitment:

> "I get it — $15K is real money and you want to make sure it's the right move. That's exactly why we start with Architecture instead of jumping straight to the full build. You see everything before you commit to more. It's designed to be low-risk for exactly this reason."

#### The Scarcity Close (Use Sparingly and Only When True)

> "Fair warning — Corey takes on 2-3 new Architecture partners per quarter because he works with every one personally. We're currently [X spots] into this quarter. I'm not trying to rush you, but I want to be transparent about capacity."

Only say this if it's true. Manufacturing false scarcity destroys trust permanently.

---

### What NOT to Do When Closing

**Never say:**
- "So, what do you think?" (Too vague — gives them an easy out)
- "Take your time, no pressure." (Removes all urgency)
- "I'll send over some info and you can decide." (No next step)
- "Any questions?" as your final line (Puts the ball in their court with no direction)

**Always end with:**
- A specific next step
- A specific date and time
- A sent calendar invite (before hanging up)

## Presentation Delivery Tips

### Energy and Pace

- **Start with energy.** The opening sets the tone. If you're flat, they'll be flat.
- **Slow down during the investment section.** Numbers need time to land. Don't rush through pricing.
- **Speed up during demos.** Demos should feel dynamic and alive, not like a tutorial.
- **Pause after big statements.** "You're losing $180K a year." Pause. Let it sink in.

### Screen Sharing Best Practices

- **Share only the relevant window.** Not your entire desktop. Not your email inbox.
- **Use your cursor as a pointer.** Slowly guide their eyes to what matters.
- **Don't read your slides.** If you have slides, they should be visual — not walls of text.
- **Face the camera when talking.** Look at the demo when demonstrating. Look at the camera when making a point.

### Body Language (On Video)

- **Sit up straight.** Posture communicates confidence.
- **Lean slightly forward** when they're talking. Shows engagement.
- **Nod when they make a point.** Visual confirmation that you're listening.
- **Use hands naturally.** Frozen hands look stiff. Wild gestures look unhinged. Natural movement looks confident.
- **Smile when appropriate.** Not constantly — that's weird. But when they share a win or you share an exciting possibility, let it show.

## When the Presentation Goes Off Track

It will happen. Prospects go on tangents, ask unexpected questions, or bring up new problems you didn't uncover in discovery. Here's how to handle it:

### They Bring Up a New Problem

> "That's really important — I'm glad you brought it up. Let me note that down because it changes what I'd recommend. [Note it.] Here's how the Architecture phase would account for that..."

Don't ignore it. Don't say "let's park that." Integrate it.

### They Get Skeptical

> "I appreciate the honesty. What specifically are you concerned about? I'd rather address it directly than leave it hanging."

Skepticism is healthy. It means they're engaged. Meet it with transparency, not defensiveness.

### They Compare You to a Competitor

> "Great question. The biggest difference is [one specific thing]. But honestly, the best way to compare is to see what the Architecture phase produces for your specific situation. Nobody else offers that level of customization at the starting phase."

One differentiator is more powerful than a list of five. Pick your strongest and lean into it.

### They Ask for a Discount

> "I understand the instinct to negotiate, and I respect that. The $15K reflects the level of CEO involvement and the depth of the architecture. We don't discount — but I can tell you the ROI math makes this a no-brainer within the first month. Let me show you why..."

Never discount. It devalues the work and sets a precedent for every future conversation.

---

## Key Takeaway

The presentation is where discovery meets solution. Your job is to reflect their pain back to them, show them a better future through live demos and real results, and make the Architecture phase feel like the obvious, low-risk next step. Lead with their words, show outcomes over features, and never present price without context. The close should feel like a natural conclusion to a great conversation — not a high-pressure moment.

## What to Practice Today

1. **Practice the opening reconnection** — "Last time you mentioned..." — using notes from a real or hypothetical discovery call. It should feel natural, not memorized.
2. **Run through the ROI framing** out loud. Practice presenting the cost of doing nothing, the cost of alternatives, and the Architecture investment. The numbers should flow without hesitation.
3. **Practice the "What would need to be true?" question** 5 times. Out loud. Get comfortable with the silence that follows.
4. **Watch a recording** of a past presentation (yours or a team member's). Note: where did energy dip? Where did the prospect lean in? Where was there an opportunity to go deeper?
