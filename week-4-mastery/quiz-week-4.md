## Section A: Objection Handling (Questions 1-5)

### Question 1

A prospect says: "I need to think about it." What is your FIRST response?

- A) "Sure, take your time. I'll follow up next week."
- B) "What specifically needs more thought? Is it the investment, the timing, or something else?"
- C) "I understand. Let me send you some more information."
- D) "We have a special offer that expires Friday."

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B) "What specifically needs more thought? Is it the investment, the timing, or something else?"**

**Why this matters:** "I need to think about it" is almost always a polite way of saying there's an unspoken concern. Giving them three categories (investment, timing, something else) makes it easy for them to reveal the real issue. Option A lets them escape without resolving anything. Option C adds information they didn't ask for. Option D is pressure selling.
</details>

### Question 2

A prospect says: "$15K is too expensive." You should:

- A) Offer a 20% discount to close the deal today
- B) Anchor against the $200K+ hiring alternative, then reframe as investment
- C) Agree and suggest they start with a cheaper competitor
- D) Tell them the price is non-negotiable and move on

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Anchor against the $200K+ hiring alternative, then reframe as investment**

**Why this matters:** Price objections are value perception problems, not price problems. Anchoring against the expensive alternative ($200K+ hiring cost) makes $15K sound like a fraction. You never discount (A), never validate their objection by agreeing (C), and never dismiss them (D). Reframe the number, don't fight it.
</details>

### Question 3

When a prospect raises an objection about pricing, custom scope, or contract terms, what should you do?

- A) Handle it yourself using the ROI math from training
- B) Offer founding partner pricing to get them across the line
- C) Say "That's a great question for Corey — let me get you 20 minutes with him"
- D) Tell them you'll get back to them with a custom quote

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C) Say "That's a great question for Corey — let me get you 20 minutes with him"**

**Why this matters:** This is the non-negotiable handoff rule. You are not authorized to negotiate pricing, change scope, or modify contracts. Your job is to fill the funnel. Corey closes. This isn't a limitation — it's your superpower. You never have to feel pressured to answer questions above your role.
</details>

### Question 4

A prospect says: "We tried AI tools before and they didn't work." The real psychology behind this objection is:

- A) They hate technology
- B) They've been burned and are skeptical of all AI claims
- C) They can't afford your solution
- D) They don't understand what AI is

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B) They've been burned and are skeptical of all AI claims**

**Why this matters:** Past negative experience creates a strong resistance frame. This prospect is NOT anti-technology — they tried it, which means they were open to it. They're skeptical because the last experience failed. Your response needs empathy first ("common experience"), then differentiation ("templates vs. custom"). Address the specific failure, not AI in general.
</details>

### Question 5

What is the "meta-rule" of objection handling?

- A) Always have a script ready for every possible objection
- B) The objection they state is rarely the real objection — always dig one level deeper
- C) Handle every objection in under 30 seconds
- D) Never acknowledge an objection — redirect to benefits immediately

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B) The objection they state is rarely the real objection — always dig one level deeper**

**Why this matters:** "Too expensive" usually means "I don't understand the value." "I need to think about it" usually means "I have an unspoken concern." Surface objections are doors — the real issue is behind them. The technique: "I hear you on [surface objection]. Is it specifically about [that], or is there something else on your mind?"
</details>

## Section B: Pricing & ROI (Questions 6-10)

### Question 6

What is the correct annual value equation for the AI OS?

- A) $15K x 12 months = $180K annual value
- B) 20 hrs/week saved x $100/hr x 52 weeks = $104K/year in recovered value
- C) $2,500/month x 12 = $30K annual value
- D) Average deal size x close rate = annual value

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B) 20 hrs/week saved x $100/hr x 52 weeks = $104K/year in recovered value**

**Why this matters:** This is the core ROI equation you need memorized. If the AI OS saves 20 hours per week at the owner's time value of $100/hour, that's $104K/year in recovered value — nearly a 7x return on a $15K investment. This is the number that makes $15K sound like a no-brainer.
</details>

### Question 7

When presenting pricing, what is the correct order?

- A) Price → Value → Payment terms → ROI
- B) ROI → Price → Value → Anchor
- C) Recap pain → Present value → Anchor high → Present price → Frame payment → State ROI → Pause
- D) Anchor → Discount → Close

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C) Recap pain → Present value → Anchor high → Present price → Frame payment → State ROI → Pause**

**Why this matters:** The 7-step sequence is designed to create maximum context before the number hits. Recap their pain (make it personal), present the value (what they get), anchor high ($200K alternative), THEN present price ($15K). Frame the payment (50/50) and state the ROI. Then pause — don't fill the silence. This sequence makes $15K feel inevitable, not shocking.
</details>

### Question 8

A prospect can't afford the $15K Architecture phase. What do you do?

- A) Offer a 50% discount
- B) Suggest they start with a free consultation
- C) Explain that price is fixed but scope is flexible, and hand off to Corey for options
- D) Tell them to come back when they have the budget

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C) Explain that price is fixed but scope is flexible, and hand off to Corey for options**

**Why this matters:** Never negotiate on price — negotiate on scope. Discounting (A) signals the original price was inflated. But dismissing them (D) loses a potential client. The right move is to acknowledge the constraint, mention that a focused engagement at different scope might work, and hand off to Corey to determine the right solution. Founding partner pricing exists but is ONLY offered by Corey.
</details>

### Question 9

Which word should you NEVER use when discussing pricing?

- A) Investment
- B) Cost
- C) Retainer
- D) Value

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Cost**

**Why this matters:** "Cost" implies money leaving. "Investment" implies money working. This isn't semantic games — it genuinely changes how the prospect's brain processes the number. Same with "fee" (feels like a tax) vs. "retainer" (feels like securing access). Words create frames, and frames determine decisions.
</details>

### Question 10

The management retainer is $2,500/month. What is the correct anchor comparison?

- A) "It's like a Netflix subscription for your business"
- B) "It's less than a part-time employee who works 24/7 including weekends"
- C) "It's only $83 a day"
- D) "It's half of what other agencies charge"

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B) "It's less than a part-time employee who works 24/7 including weekends"**

**Why this matters:** The best anchor compares to the actual alternative — hiring. A part-time marketing coordinator costs $3,500-4,000/month. A part-time developer costs $5,000-8,000/month. At $2,500/month, the AI OS retainer replaces both AND works around the clock. Netflix comparisons (A) trivialize the product. Daily breakdowns (C) make it sound like a subscription. Competitor comparisons (D) invite price shopping.
</details>

## Section C: Pipeline & Metrics (Questions 11-15)

### Question 11

How many minutes should your daily pipeline routine take?

- A) 5 minutes
- B) 15 minutes
- C) 45 minutes
- D) 2 hours

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B) 15 minutes**

**Why this matters:** Pipeline management isn't a half-day activity. It's 15 minutes every morning: check follow-ups due, scan for stale deals, send follow-ups, update records, and (at end of day) share a summary with Corey. Consistency beats intensity. Those 15 minutes compound into closed deals over weeks and months.

### Question 12

What is the maximum number of days a prospect should sit in the same pipeline stage without a touchpoint?

- A) 7 days
- B) 14 days
- C) 30 days
- D) There's no maximum — patience is key

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B) 14 days**

**Why this matters:** No prospect should sit in the same stage for more than 14 days without a touchpoint. After 14 days of silence, they need either a break-up message, a move to Nurture, or a move to Closed Lost. Stale deals clog your pipeline, waste mental energy, and create the illusion of a healthy pipeline when it's actually dying.
</details>

What is the weekly target for outreach messages sent?

- B) 5

<summary>Answer</summary>

That feeds 1 discovery call per week, which compounds to 4+ discovery calls per month. The math works backward from the close target — 5 outreach per week is the minimum activity that sustains a healthy pipeline.

### Question 14

What is the target close rate from proposal to signed deal?

- A) 10%
- B) 25%
- C) 40%
- D) 75%

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C) 40%**

**Why this matters:** A 40% close rate on proposals means that out of every 5 proposals sent, 2 become clients. This is achievable because by the time you send a proposal, you've already qualified the prospect through discovery. You're not proposing to everyone — you're proposing to people who are a genuine fit. If your close rate is below 40%, you're likely sending proposals too early or to unqualified prospects.

## Section D: Advanced Techniques (Questions 16-20)

### Question 16

In Chris Voss's negotiation framework, "mirroring" means:

- A) Copying the prospect's body language
- B) Repeating the last 1-3 words they said with an upward inflection
- C) Agreeing with everything they say
- D) Sending them a follow-up that mirrors their email format

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Repeating the last 1-3 words they said with an upward inflection**

**Why this matters:** Mirroring triggers a subconscious need to elaborate. When you repeat someone's last few words as a question, they feel compelled to explain further. You get 10x more information with two words than you would with a direct question. It's the simplest and most powerful technique in your toolkit.
</details>

### Question 17

What is an "accusation audit"?

- A) A financial review of the prospect's business
- B) Listing every negative thing the prospect might think about you before they say it
- C) A formal complaint process for unhappy clients
- D) Reviewing a prospect's online reviews for negative feedback

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Listing every negative thing the prospect might think about you before they say it**

**Why this matters:** By voicing their fears first ("You're probably thinking this is another sales call..."), you take the sting out. It's counterintuitive — but psychologically, it disarms them because you've shown you understand their perspective. The negatives lose their power when you name them first.
</details>

### Question 19

The "assumptive close" works by:

- A) Assuming the prospect will say no and preparing for it
- B) Asking a yes/no question about moving forward
- C) Stating the next step as if it's already decided and offering a choice of timing
- D) Assuming the deal is closed and sending the contract without asking

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C) Stating the next step as if it's already decided and offering a choice of timing**

**Why this matters:** Instead of "Would you like to schedule a call with Corey?" (invites "no"), you say "I'm going to set up a call with Corey — does Thursday afternoon or Friday morning work better?" The yes is assumed. The choice is about when, not whether. This maintains momentum and keeps the conversation moving forward naturally.
</details>
