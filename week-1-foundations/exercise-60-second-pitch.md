# Exercise: The 60-Second Pitch

**Week 1 Capstone Exercise | Objective: Craft and deliver a 60-second pitch that you'd trust enough to sell to your own business**

---

## The Assignment

This is not optional. This is the single most important exercise of Week 1.

By the end of this exercise, you will have a pitch that:
- Explains what Nichefinders AI does in plain English
- Creates urgency without being pushy
- Includes a specific ROI mention
- Ends with a clear call to action
- Sounds like YOU — not a script

### The Steps

**Step 1: Study the three sample pitches below.** Don't memorize them. Absorb the structure, the rhythm, and the logic. Notice how each one follows the same pattern: hook, problem, solution, proof, call to action.

**Step 2: Write your own 60-second pitch.** Use the structure but make it yours. Use words you'd actually say. If something sounds like a brochure, rewrite it until it sounds like a conversation.

**Step 3: Record it on your phone.** Stand up. Speak at a natural pace. Don't read — deliver it like you're talking to a real person.

**Step 4: Listen to the recording.** Ask yourself:
- Would I trust this person to sell to MY business?
- Does it sound rehearsed or natural?
- Is there specific ROI or is it all vague promises?
- Is there a clear next step at the end?

**Step 5: Re-record until it passes.** Most people need 5-8 takes. That's normal. Each take gets better.

**Step 6: Submit your best recording to Corey.** He'll provide feedback and approve you for Week 2.

## The Structure (Your Blueprint)

Every effective 60-second pitch follows this 5-part structure:

```
1. HOOK (5 seconds)
   → Grab attention with a question or bold statement

2. PROBLEM (15 seconds)
   → Describe the pain they're living with — specifically

3. SOLUTION (15 seconds)
   → What we do and why it's different

4. PROOF (15 seconds)
   → Specific result, client example, or ROI number

5. CALL TO ACTION (10 seconds)
   → Clear, low-pressure next step
```

Total: ~60 seconds at a natural speaking pace (~150 words).

## Sample Pitch 1: General (Any Industry)

Use this when you don't know the prospect's industry yet — networking events, cold introductions, or when someone asks "What do you do?"

> **HOOK:** "Can I ask you something? How many hours a week do you spend on tasks that don't actually grow your business?"
>
> **PROBLEM:** "Most business owners I talk to are working 50, 60, sometimes 70 hours a week — and 80% of that time is spent on things like scheduling, follow-ups, chasing leads, managing their team. They built the business for freedom, but the business became the hardest job they've ever had."
>
> **SOLUTION:** "That's what we fix. Nichefinders AI builds custom AI operating systems for businesses. We map your entire operation, automate the repetitive stuff, and install an AI layer that handles lead capture, scheduling, follow-ups, and communications — 24 hours a day. And unlike SaaS platforms, you own everything we build. No subscriptions. No lock-in."
>
> **PROOF:** "One of our clients was losing $50,000 a year in missed calls alone. After we installed their AI OS, every call gets answered, every lead gets followed up, and the owner checks a 2-minute morning brief instead of spending 90 minutes catching up. The system paid for itself in the first quarter."
>
> **CTA:** "I don't know if it'd be a fit for your business, but I'd love to learn more about what you do. Do you have 15 minutes this week for a quick call?"

**Word count:** ~185 words. At natural pace, this runs about 65-70 seconds. Trim as needed for your delivery speed.

## Sample Pitch 2: Contractor-Specific

Use this when talking to a plumber, HVAC company, roofer, electrician, or general contractor.

> **HOOK:** "Quick question — when a homeowner calls your company at 7 PM because their AC just died, who answers the phone?"
>
> **PROBLEM:** "For most contractors, the answer is nobody. Or a voicemail. And that homeowner calls the next company on Google. Every missed call is a job that goes to your competitor. And it's not just after-hours — during the day your team is too busy dispatching, scheduling, and chasing down paperwork to follow up with every lead. The calls come in fast, but the follow-up falls apart."
>
> **SOLUTION:** "We build AI systems specifically for contractors. Your phone gets answered on the first ring — by an AI that sounds natural, qualifies the caller, and books the appointment. Follow-ups happen automatically. Scheduling and dispatch run on autopilot. And you control the whole thing from your phone. Text a question, get an answer. Give a command, it's done."
>
> **PROOF:** "We work with Fox Valley Plumbing & Backflow — they were missing about a third of their calls. Now every single call is captured, day and night. Their office manager got 10 hours a week back. And the owner doesn't even check voicemail anymore — the AI handles it."
>
> **CTA:** "I'd be happy to show you exactly what that looks like for your business. Would a 15-minute call work sometime this week?"

**Word count:** ~200 words. Runs about 70 seconds. Natural for contractor conversations where you want to paint a vivid picture.

## Sample Pitch 3: Agency / Professional Services

Use this when talking to another agency, consultant, accounting firm, law firm, or professional services company.

> **HOOK:** "How much time does your team spend each month on client reporting?"
>
> **PROBLEM:** "For most agencies, it's 5 to 10 hours per client. That's not billable time — it's a tax on your team's capacity. And it's not just reporting. It's onboarding that's different every time. It's content calendars that take days to build. It's project management that lives in 4 different tools. You're spending more time managing the work than doing the work your clients actually pay you for."
>
> **SOLUTION:** "We build AI operating systems for service businesses. Automated reporting that generates itself. Content pipelines that produce drafts without your team lifting a finger. Onboarding workflows that are consistent every time. And everything connects — your CRM, your project tools, your calendar — into one system. You own it all. No SaaS fees. No vendor lock-in."
>
> **PROOF:** "Our average client reclaims 15-20 hours per week in admin time. That's the equivalent of a full-time hire — without the salary. For an agency doing $2M in revenue, that kind of efficiency isn't just nice to have. It's the difference between 15% margins and 35% margins."
>
> **CTA:** "If you're open to it, I'd love to do a quick 15-minute audit of where AI could plug into your operations. No pitch, just an honest look at what's possible."

**Word count:** ~195 words. Runs about 68 seconds. Calibrated for a more sophisticated audience that thinks in terms of margins and efficiency.

## The 30-Second Version (Networking / Elevator)

Sometimes you don't have 60 seconds. Someone at a networking event asks "What do you do?" and you have 30 seconds before the conversation moves on.

### The Formula

```
We help [WHO] stop [PAIN] by building [SOLUTION] that [RESULT].
```

### General Version

> "We build custom AI operating systems for small businesses — typically service companies doing $1M to $10M. Most of them are working 60 hours a week and losing leads because they can't keep up. We automate the repetitive stuff — answering calls, follow-ups, scheduling, communications — so the owner actually runs the business instead of the business running them. One client was losing $50K a year in missed calls. Now every call is captured, 24/7."

### Even Shorter (15 seconds — "the handshake")

> "We build AI systems that run small businesses. Answers their phones, follows up with leads, automates their operations. The owner gets their time back and the business runs whether they're there or not."

## The Grading Rubric

Record your pitch and grade yourself honestly. Have a friend or colleague grade you too.

### Category 1: Confidence (1-5)

| Score | Description |
|-------|------------|
| 1 | Nervous, uncertain, lots of filler words ("um," "like," "you know") |
| 2 | Hesitant, speaks in a questioning tone, sounds unsure of the product |
| 3 | Adequate — gets through it but doesn't sound fully committed |
| 4 | Confident — speaks with conviction, few hesitations, good pace |
| 5 | Commanding — sounds like someone who's closed 100 deals and believes every word |

**How to improve:** Record yourself 10 times. The first 3 will be shaky. By take 7, you'll start to own it. Confidence comes from repetition, not talent.

### Category 2: Clarity (1-5)

| Score | Description |
|-------|------------|
| 1 | Confusing, jumps around, prospect wouldn't understand what you do |
| 2 | Somewhat clear, but includes jargon or unclear explanations |
| 3 | Clear enough — the prospect gets the general idea |
| 4 | Very clear — no jargon, logical flow, easy to follow |
| 5 | Crystal clear — a 12-year-old could understand what you do and why it matters |

**How to improve:** After recording, play it for someone who knows nothing about AI. Ask them "What do I do?" If their answer matches what you intended, you're at a 4 or 5. If they're confused, simplify.

### Category 3: Specificity (1-5)

| Score | Description |
|-------|------------|
| 1 | All vague claims ("we help businesses grow," "we save you time") |
| 2 | One vague number or example, mostly generic |
| 3 | Includes one specific number or client example |
| 4 | Includes specific ROI number AND a client reference or scenario |
| 5 | Uses a specific dollar amount, a named client example or vivid scenario, and connects it to the prospect's world |

**How to improve:** Replace every vague word with a specific one. "Save time" becomes "reclaim 15 hours a week." "Get more leads" becomes "capture the 30% of calls going to voicemail." Specificity is a discipline, not a talent.

### Category 4: Call to Action (1-5)

| Score | Description |
|-------|------------|
| 1 | No CTA — the pitch just... ends |
| 2 | Vague CTA ("let me know if you're interested") |
| 3 | CTA exists but feels pushy or awkward |
| 4 | Clear, low-pressure CTA with a specific next step |
| 5 | CTA feels natural, creates urgency without pressure, and makes saying "yes" easy |

**How to improve:** The best CTA is specific and low-commitment. "Would a 15-minute call work this week?" beats "Let's schedule a meeting." "I'd love to show you what this looks like for your business" beats "Are you ready to get started?"

### Scoring

| Total Score | Result |
|-------------|--------|
| 20 | Elite. You're ready to sell. |
| 18-19 | Strong. Minor polish needed. |
| 16-17 | Passing. Focus on your weakest category. |
| 13-15 | Not ready. Practice another 10 takes and re-grade. |
| Below 13 | Go back to the modules. You're missing foundational understanding. |

**Minimum to pass: 16 points with no category below 3.**

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Starting with "We"
Don't start with your company. Start with the prospect's world.

**Bad:** "We're Nichefinders AI and we build custom AI systems..."
**Good:** "How many hours a week do you spend on tasks that don't grow your business?"

### 2. Feature Dumping
Don't list features. Tell a story.

**Bad:** "We have voice agents, chatbots, workflow automation, CRM integration, reporting dashboards..."
**Good:** "Your phone gets answered on the first ring. Every lead gets followed up. Your morning starts with a 2-minute brief instead of 90 minutes of catch-up."

### 3. No Numbers
If your pitch doesn't include at least one specific number, it's too vague.

**Bad:** "Our clients see great results."
**Good:** "One client was losing $50,000 a year in missed calls. Now every call is captured, 24/7."

### 4. Weak Close
If your pitch ends with "so... yeah" or "let me know if you want to learn more," you've wasted everything that came before it.

**Bad:** "So yeah, that's what we do. Let me know."
**Good:** "I'd be happy to show you exactly what that looks like for your business. Would a 15-minute call work this week?"

### 5. Trying to Close Too Hard
The opposite problem. Don't go from 0 to "so are you ready to invest $15,000?" in 60 seconds.

**Bad:** "Our Architecture phase starts at $15K — when do you want to get started?"
**Good:** "I don't know if it's a fit yet, but I'd love to learn more about your business. Got 15 minutes this week?"

## Pitch Development Workflow

Here's the process for developing your personal pitch over the next 48 hours:

### Hour 1: Write
- Read all three sample pitches twice
- Write your own version following the 5-part structure
- Don't worry about perfection — just get words on paper

### Hour 2: Refine
- Read your pitch out loud. Circle anything that sounds unnatural.
- Replace jargon with plain English
- Make sure you have at least one specific number and one client reference
- Verify your CTA is clear and low-pressure

### Hour 3: Record (First Round)
- Record 5 takes
- Listen to all 5 without judgment
- Pick the best one
- Note: pace (too fast? too slow?), filler words, energy level

### Hour 4: Polish (Next Day)
- Record 5 more takes with adjustments
- Grade yourself using the rubric
- Get feedback from someone (friend, family, colleague)
- Record your final version

### Hour 5: Submit
- Send your best recording to Corey
- Include your self-grade (the 4 categories and total)
- Include one sentence about what you'd improve

---

## After You Submit

Once Corey approves your pitch:

1. **Practice it daily.** Every morning for the next week, deliver your pitch once — out loud, standing up. Keep it fresh.

2. **Adapt it.** When you learn a prospect's industry before a call, swap in industry-specific language from Module 05. Same structure, different vocabulary.

3. **Evolve it.** As you close your first deals, replace the sample proof points with YOUR proof points. Nothing sells better than "I just did this for a company exactly like yours."

4. **Time it.** Your pitch should clock between 55-70 seconds. Under 55 means you're rushing. Over 70 means you're rambling. Find the sweet spot.

---

## Key Takeaway

> **Your pitch is not a script you memorize — it's a conversation you've internalized.** The words should change slightly every time you say it. The structure stays the same. The confidence comes not from perfect words but from deep understanding of the product, the pain it solves, and the value it creates. When you truly understand what you're selling, the pitch takes care of itself.
